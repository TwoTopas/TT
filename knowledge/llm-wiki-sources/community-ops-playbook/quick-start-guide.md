## 2. How to Use the Templates

The templates are designed to work as a system, not a pile of random spreadsheets. There's a logical flow to how they connect.

### The General Workflow

**Step 1: Start with the Launch Checklist**

Don't skip this one. Before you do anything else, open the Community Launch Checklist and work through it. It walks you through platform setup, content prep, role creation, and the hundred other things you'll forget if you don't write them down. Mark off items as you go. This single template will save you from launching a half-baked community.

**Step 2: Set up your onboarding flow**

Open the Member Onboarding Flow template next. Your first 14 days with a new member determine whether they stick around for month two. Map out your welcome sequence day by day. What message do they get on day 1? What do you want them to do on day 3? What's the "aha moment" that makes them think *this community is for me*?

**Step 3: Plan your content**

The Weekly Content Calendar is where you'll spend most of your planning time. Block out your posting themes for the week. Educational content on Monday. Community spotlights on Wednesday. Light engagement on Friday. The template keeps you consistent, and consistency is what builds a habit in your members' minds.

**Step 4: Track who's engaging**

Once you have members, open the Engagement Score Tracker. This is your early warning system. When someone's score drops, you'll know before they go completely silent. The Member Segmentation Matrix works alongside it — it helps you group members by behavior so you can give your power users more responsibility and your at-risk members more attention.

**Step 5: Measure what matters**

The KPI Dashboard pulls everything together. Update it weekly. Watch the trends. If engagement is down, check your content calendar — are you posting consistently? If churn is up, check your onboarding flow — are new members getting a good first week?

### Tips for Working with the Templates

- **Don't fill everything out at once.** Start with the Launch Checklist and Onboarding Flow. Add the Content Calendar when you're about to start posting. Bring in the rest as you grow.
- **Update them weekly.** Set aside 30 minutes every Friday to update your templates. Thirty minutes of admin saves you hours of "what was I supposed to do?" during the week.
- **They're Google Sheets for a reason.** Share them with your team, co-host, or moderator. Multiple people can work in them at the same time.
- **The sample data is your friend.** Every template comes with pre-filled examples. You can overwrite them, but read them first — they show you what good looks like.

## 3. Template-by-Template Quick Guide

### Community Launch Checklist

Before you invite a single person to your community, you need a plan. This template is your pre-flight checklist — 30+ tasks organized into phases: foundation, platform setup, content preparation, team training, and launch day.

Start here and work through it methodically. Each task has an owner, a deadline, and a status column so you know exactly where you stand. The sample data shows you what a healthy pre-launch looks like: platform configured two weeks before launch, first week of content ready seven days before, moderators trained and assigned three days before.

**Pro tip:** Add a "launch day panic" buffer. Things always take longer than you expect. Build in three extra days for platform setup alone — Discord server configuration always has more settings than you remember.

### Member Onboarding Flow

First impressions matter, and in a community, the first impression spans days, not seconds. This template maps out a 14-day welcome sequence with columns for the day number, the action you take, the channel where it happens, the copy you use, and the goal metric you're trying to move.

A good onboarding sequence looks something like this:

- **Day 1:** Welcome message + introduce yourself thread
- **Day 2:** "Start here" guide pinned in your welcome channel
- **Day 3:** Community guidelines and role selection
- **Day 4:** First engagement prompt — a poll, an icebreaker question
- **Day 5:** Member spotlight or showcase of what's possible
- **Day 6:** Calendar of upcoming events
- **Day 7:** Ask for feedback and set expectations for week two

The template handles this structure for you. Just plug in your specific copy and channels.

**Pro tip:** New members who complete a profile and make their first post within 48 hours are significantly more likely to stick around. Design your day 1 and day 2 actions to push toward that first interaction.

### Weekly Content Calendar

This is the template you'll use most often. It's a simple 7-day grid with columns for daily theme, channel, content type, author, and status.

Structure your week around themes so members know what to expect:

- **Monday:** Educational content — tutorials, guides, resources
- **Tuesday:** Community spotlights — member wins, showcases, shoutouts
- **Wednesday:** Interactive — AMAs, Q&As, polls, challenges
- **Thursday:** Behind the scenes — culture posts, team updates
- **Friday:** Weekly roundup — best of the week, weekend preview
- **Weekend:** Light engagement — off-topic, games, casual chat

The sample data in the template gives you a full week of realistic posts across three channels. Use it as a starting point and adjust based on what your community actually responds to.

**Pro tip:** Batch-create a week's worth of content in one sitting. It takes about an hour and saves you from the daily "what do I post?" scramble. Monday morning you'll thank yourself.

### Engagement Score Tracker

You can't improve what you don't measure. This template assigns each member a score based on their weekly activity: posts created, reactions given, comments made, days active. The score updates automatically based on the sample data structure.

The goal is to spot trends before they become problems. When a member's score drops for two consecutive weeks, they're at risk of churning. Reach out. Send a DM. Tag them in something relevant. One personal touch can bring them back.

On the flip side, members with consistently high scores are your power users. They should be on your shortlist for ambassador programs, moderator roles, or beta-testing access.

**Pro tip:** Set a weekly threshold. Anyone below it gets a check-in. This turns engagement tracking from "maybe I should check on people" to a repeatable system.

### Member Segmentation Matrix

Not all members are the same, and treating them like they are is one of the fastest ways to lose both your power users and your newbies. This template categorizes members by tier, interests, activity level, and estimated lifetime value.

The matrix uses a simple framework:

- **VIPs:** High engagement, high value. These are your ambassadors and advocates. Give them exclusive access, early peeks, and direct lines to you.
- **Active members:** Regular participants. Nurture them toward VIP status with recognition and responsibility.
- **Casual members:** They show up sometimes. They're not a problem — they just need the right trigger to become more active.
- **At-risk:** Engagement is dropping. These members need outreach before they leave.
- **Dormant:** Not currently active. Consider a reactivation campaign or accept that they've moved on.

**Pro tip:** Review this matrix monthly. Segments shift, and you want to catch the shifts early — especially when someone moves from "active" to "at-risk."

### Referral Program Designer

Word-of-mouth is the most cost-effective growth channel for communities, but you can't just hope it happens. This template helps you structure a referral program with columns for referral source, reward structure, conversion rate tracking, and total members acquired.

A good referral program has three things: a clear incentive for the referrer, a clear incentive for the new member, and a way to track both. The template handles the tracking part — you define the incentives.

Common referral structures that work:

- **Two-sided rewards:** Referrer gets a month free, new member gets 20% off their first month
- **Tiered rewards:** 3 referrals = one month free, 5 referrals = exclusive content pack, 10 referrals = lifetime discount
- **Community goals:** Collective referral target unlocks something for everyone — a bonus AMA, a resource pack, a community event

**Pro tip:** Your best referrers are already your most engaged members. Check your Engagement Score Tracker, find your top 10%, and invite them personally to your referral program before you announce it publicly.

### Event Planning SOP

Events are one of the highest-impact activities you can run in a community, but they're also the easiest to botch. This template breaks event planning into phases — ideation, planning, promotion, execution, and post-event — with tasks, owners, timelines, and budgets for each.

The sample data walks through a real event: a 7-day challenge with daily prompts, a kickoff livestream, and a closing celebration. Use it as a template for your own events, swapping in your theme and timing.

**Pro tip:** The most common event mistake is spending all your energy on the thing itself and none on the follow-up. Your post-event phase should include: thank-you message, content recap, survey for next time, and a clear "what happens now" for attendees.

### Community KPI Dashboard

This is your community's command center. It tracks the key metrics you should be watching weekly: member count, engagement rate, retention rate, conversion rate, revenue, and more. Each metric has a current value, a target, a trend direction, and a suggested action if things are off track.

The dashboard is designed to answer one question: *Is the community healthy?* If engagement is trending down, you know to look at content and onboarding. If revenue is flat, you know to focus on monetization and upgrades.

**Pro tip:** Don't track more than 10 metrics. Everything beyond that is noise. This template keeps you focused on what matters.

### Feedback Collection System

Your members will tell you what's working and what isn't — if you make it easy for them. This template creates a structured system for collecting, prioritizing, and acting on feedback. Columns include the date, source, feedback summary, priority level, owner, and status.

The key is closing the loop. When a member gives feedback, they should see that it was heard and acted on. The template tracks this from "submitted" through "in review" to "implemented" or "declined with reason."

**Pro tip:** Every quarter, review your feedback log and look for patterns. Three people mentioning the same problem means thirty people are thinking it. That's your priority queue.

### Revenue Tracking Sheet

If your community generates revenue — subscriptions, course sales, coaching, digital products — this template tracks it. Columns for month, source, amount, subscriber count, and ARPU (average revenue per user) give you a clear picture of your community's financial health.

Track your revenue monthly and watch for trends. Is ARPU growing? Are you adding subscribers faster than you're losing them? Which revenue source has the highest margin?

**Pro tip:** The most important number on this sheet is your churn rate — how many members you lose each month. If you can reduce churn by even 5%, the impact on revenue is often bigger than adding new members. Retention is cheaper than acquisition.

## 4. Setting Up Your Community

Your templates don't live in a vacuum. They support a real community on a real platform. Here's how to set up on each of the three platforms we cover.

### Discord

Discord is the most flexible platform for communities, and it's where most community managers start. Here's the minimum viable setup:

**Channel structure:**

- **#welcome** — First channel new members see. Pinned posts: community guidelines, roles guide, start-here doc
- **#announcements** — Only you (and your mods) post here. Members get notifications. Use it sparingly.
- **#general** — Open chat. Where most conversations happen.
- **#introductions** — New members post here. Encourage everyone to share something about themselves.
- **#resources** — Pinned posts with your best content, guides, and links.
- **#events** — Upcoming events, calendar, and event recaps.
- **#voice-chat** — Voice channel for live conversations, AMAs, coworking.

**Roles to create:**

- **@Member** — Default role for everyone
- **@New Member** — Assigned for the first 7 days (gives you a window to engage before they get full access)
- **@VIP / @Premium** — For paid tiers or power users
- **@Moderator** — Trusted members who help manage the community
- **@Announcements** — Ping role for people who want notifications

**Bots that help:**

- **MEE6** — Auto-moderation, leveling, welcome messages
- **Carl-bot** — Reaction roles, reminders, moderation
- **Dyno** — Custom commands, auto-mod, logging

Start with just the bots you need. You can always add more later.

**Pro tip:** Don't open 20 channels on day one. Start with 5-6. New members find empty servers confusing. Let channel requests come from the community as it grows.

### Circle

Circle is built for paid communities and course-based memberships. It's cleaner than Discord and easier for less tech-savvy members to navigate.

**Space structure:**

- **Welcome Space** — Onboarding, guidelines, start here
- **General Discussion** — Main community conversation
- **Questions & Answers** — Structured Q&A where answers don't get buried
- **Resources & Downloads** — Your templates, guides, worksheets
- **Member Spotlight** — Wins, introductions, showcases
- **Events & Live Streams** — Calendar and recordings

**Key settings:**

- Enable member profiles so people can share about themselves
- Set up your community space with topic-based categories rather than channel chaos
- Use the course integration if you're delivering educational content — it's Circle's strongest feature

**Pro tip:** Circle's native search is better than Discord's, so make sure your posts have clear titles. Members will find old content months later if you name things well.

### Skool

Skool combines community with course delivery and gamification. It's newer than Discord and Circle, but it's growing fast — especially for paid communities.

**What Skool does well:**

- **Gamification** — Leaderboards, levels, and achievements are built in. Members can see their rank and earn points for participating.
- **Classroom format** — Discussions are threaded like a classroom, which works well for course-based communities.
- **Calendar-based delivery** — You can schedule content to release on a timeline, perfect for cohorts and challenges.
- **Native monetization** — Skool charges a $9/month platform fee but handles payments natively.

**Structure tips:**

- Organize your courses into weekly modules
- Use the classroom for lesson discussions
- Keep general chat in the main feed
- Use the calendar for live events and cohort start dates

**Pro tip:** Skool's gamification is its superpower, but leaderboards can discourage new members if they see a huge gap between them and the top. Consider resetting leaderboards quarterly or having a "rookie rank" for the first 30 days.

### Which Platform Should You Choose?

Quick decision framework:

| If you want... | Start with |
|---------------|-----------|
| Maximum flexibility, lots of channels, custom bots | Discord |
| Clean interface, paid community, course delivery | Circle |
| Built-in gamification, all-in-one, less setup | Skool |
| Simple, low-maintenance, newsletter-driven | Substack |

You don't have to pick just one. Many community managers use Discord for daily engagement and Circle or Skool for paid content and courses. The templates in this playbook work across all of them — just swap the platform name in the channel/spaces columns.

## 5. Your First 30 Days as a Community Manager

Your first month sets the tone for everything that follows. Here's a week-by-week plan.

### Week 1: Foundation

**Day 1-3: Set up your infrastructure**

- Open the Community Launch Checklist and work through it
- Set up your platform (Discord / Circle / Skool — use the guidance in section 4)
- Configure your first channels and roles
- Write your community guidelines (keep it simple: be respectful, no spam, have fun)

**Day 4-7: Prepare your content**

- Open the Weekly Content Calendar and fill out your first week of posts
- Write your welcome message and introduction prompts
- Prepare 3-5 pieces of educational content to share in week 1
- Set up your Member Onboarding Flow with day 1-7 actions

**Don't launch yet.** Week 1 is about having everything ready so when you open the doors, it runs smoothly.

### Week 2: Launch

**Day 8-10: Invite your first members**

- Start with your warmest audience: email list, Twitter followers, existing customers
- Send personal invites, not mass announcements. A DM is worth ten broadcast messages.
- Be present. When someone joins, welcome them personally. Reply to every introduction post.

**Day 11-14: Drive early engagement**

- Post your first piece of educational content
- Ask an engaging question in general chat
- Host a welcome event: a quick AMA, a kickoff call, or a challenge
- Check your Engagement Score Tracker at the end of the week. Who's participating? Who's quiet?

**Your goal for week 2:** Get every new member to make at least one post. That first interaction is everything.

### Week 3: Build Momentum

**Day 15-18: Establish your rhythm**

- Settle into your weekly content themes from the Content Calendar
- Start a recurring thread: "Monday Wins" or "Friday Feedback"
- Identify your first power users — the people who are engaging most — and reach out personally to thank them

**Day 19-21: Collect feedback**

- Post your first feedback request: "What's working? What's not?"
- Log everything in the Feedback Collection System
- Act on at least one piece of feedback immediately and announce it — this shows you're listening

**Your goal for week 3:** Members should start to feel like the community has a rhythm. They know what to expect and when.

### Week 4: Optimize

**Day 22-25: Review your metrics**

- Open the Community KPI Dashboard and fill in your first month of data
- Look at your engagement rate. Compare it to your targets.
- Check your onboarding flow. Are new members completing the welcome sequence?

**Day 26-30: Plan month two**

- What worked in month one? Do more of it.
- What didn't work? Cut it or change it.
- Introduce one new initiative: a member spotlight, a weekly challenge, or an upcoming event
- Start planning your first community event using the Event Planning SOP

**Your goal for week 4:** You should have enough data to make informed decisions about month two. Don't guess — use the KPI Dashboard and Engagement Tracker.

### Red Flags to Watch For in Month One

- **Silent launches.** You opened the doors but nobody came. Go back to personal invites and direct outreach.
- **You're doing all the talking.** If you're the only one posting, you haven't given members a reason to engage. Ask better questions. Create lower-friction prompts like polls and reactions.
- **Churn before day 14.** If people are leaving in the first two weeks, your onboarding sequence needs work. Check your Member Onboarding Flow and make sure new members hit an "aha moment" early.
- **Toxic behavior.** Shut it down immediately, publicly, and clearly. Your community's culture is set in the first 30 days, and what you tolerate becomes the norm.
