# Chapter 6: Monetization Frameworks

**Purpose:** A community that doesn't generate revenue is a hobby, not a business. But charging your community — especially if you've built it around free access — feels uncomfortable for most operators. This chapter covers pricing models that work for Western audiences, conversion sequences that don't feel slimy, and upsell strategies that actually add value instead of just extracting it.

---

## Quick Wins — Do These Today

1. **Choose your pricing model.** Subscription, tiered, or freemium + products. Pick one based on your community type. You can change later.
2. **Set your price.** If unsure, start at $29/month. Too low leaves money on the table. Too high kills conversion. $29 is the sweet spot.
3. **Write your paid tier description.** List 3-5 specific benefits. No vague language. Compare it directly to your free tier.

---

## Why Free Communities Don't Stay Free

There's a myth in the community space that charging for access ruins the culture. The reality is the opposite. Free communities tend to decline in quality over time because:

- Free attracts passive consumers, not active contributors
- Without a barrier to entry, noise increases and signal decreases
- You, the operator, eventually burn out because you're working for free
- Members don't value what they don't pay for — even a small payment increases engagement and retention

The communities that last are the ones with a sustainable economic model. That doesn't mean you need to charge everyone. But you do need *someone* to pay for the value the community creates.

## Pricing Models: Subscription, Tiered, and Freemium

There are three primary pricing models for communities. Each one works best in different contexts.

### Subscription (Single Tier)

One price, one level of access. Simple, clean, easy to communicate.

**Best for:** Niche professional communities, accountability groups, communities where the main value is ongoing access to you or your network.

**Typical price range:** $9-49/month

**Pros:**
- Easy to understand and communicate
- Simple to manage — no complex tier logic
- Predictable revenue (MRR)
- Low friction to upgrade (there's only one option)

**Cons:**
- No upgrade path within the community
- Price sensitivity is higher — $29/month might feel expensive to someone who only wants basic access
- Hard to serve both beginners and power users with the same offering

**Example:** "The Marketing Ops Club" — $29/month. One community, one price. Members get access to all channels, monthly workshops, and a resource library. No tiers, no confusion. At 200 members, that's $5,800/month.

### Tiered Pricing (The Engagement Layer Model)

Multiple price points for different levels of access. This is the model covered in Chapter 2.

**Best for:** Communities with diverse member needs, creator communities, communities with both free and paid value.

**Typical structure:**
| Tier | Price | Best For |
|------|-------|----------|
| Free | $0 | New members, lurkers, value demonstration |
| Core | $19-29/mo | Engaged members who want full access |
| Premium | $49-99/mo | Power users who want direct access to you |

**Pros:**
- Serves different segments without forcing everyone into one bucket
- Clear upgrade path (members grow into higher tiers)
- Higher revenue potential (some members self-select into premium)
- Free tier acts as a marketing channel

**Cons:**
- More complex to manage
- Requires clear value differentiation between tiers
- Risk of cannibalization if free tier is too generous

**Pro tip:** Your free tier should be valuable enough to attract members but limited enough to create desire for the paid tier. If your free tier includes everything a paid member gets, nobody will pay.

### Freemium Community + Paid Product

The community is free, but you monetize through products, services, or content related to the community topic.

**Best for:** Content creators, course creators, consultants, SaaS companies.

**Typical structure:**
- Community: Free
- Course: $197-497 one-time
- Coaching: $1,000-5,000 program
- Templates/tools: $29-79 one-time

**Pros:**
- Maximum reach (no paywall = more members)
- Multiple revenue streams (not dependent on membership alone)
- Lower chrisk of "community fatigue" (members don't feel nickled-and-dimed)
- Free community serves as a sales funnel for higher-ticket offers

**Cons:**
- Less predictable revenue (depends on product launches)
- Requires you to create and maintain products beyond the community
- Some members will never buy anything (and that's okay, but it means lower conversion)
- Need to balance community value with commercial offers

**Example:** A free Discord server for Notion users. 5,000 members. The community is free. Revenue comes from selling Notion templates ($39-79 each), a course on advanced Notion workflows ($297), and a certification program ($497). The community feeds all three revenue streams.

### Which Model Should You Pick?

| Your Situation | Best Model | Why |
|---------------|------------|-----|
| You're a coach or consultant | Tiered ($49-99/mo) | Premium access to you is the value |
| You sell courses | Freemium + Products | Community supports course sales |
| You have a SaaS product | Freemium (free) | Community reduces churn, supports retention |
| You want simple recurring revenue | Subscription ($19-29/mo) | Predictable, easy to manage |
| You have a mixed audience (beginners + pros) | Tiered | Different tiers for different needs |

## Converting Free Members to Paid

Getting someone to pay for something they've been getting for free requires a deliberate conversion sequence. Here's what actually works.

### The Value-First Conversion Sequence

This is a 4-step sequence that runs over the first 30-60 days of a free member's journey.

**Step 1: Demonstrate Value (Days 1-14)**

Before you ask for money, make sure the free member has experienced genuine value. Not "we have nice conversations" value — specific, tangible value.

- Did they get a question answered?
- Did they find a useful resource?
- Did they make a meaningful connection?
- Did they learn something they can apply?

Track this by monitoring their engagement. If they've had 3+ positive interactions in their first 14 days, they're ready for step 2.

**Step 2: Tease the Ceiling (Days 15-30)**

Make the free member aware that there's more behind the door. Not through aggressive promotion — through natural exposure.

- Mention something from the paid tier in conversation: "We actually went deep on this in last month's paid workshop. The recording's in the vault."
- Share a clip or excerpt from paid content in a free channel with a link to the full version.
- When a free member asks a question you've covered extensively in paid content, offer a preview: "Great question — I put together a full walkthrough on this. Here's a 2-minute clip. The full version is in the paid library."

**The principle:** Don't describe the paid tier's value. Demonstrate it with a sample, then let the ceiling create its own desire.

**Step 3: The Personal Invitation (Day 30-45)**

A direct, personal invitation outperforms any automated email or announcement post. Send a DM:

> "Hey [name], I've noticed you've been active in the community this past month — great to have you here. I wanted to personally invite you to check out the paid tier. Based on what you're working on, I think you'd get a lot out of [specific paid benefit]. No pressure at all, but here's a link if you want to explore: [link]"

**Why this works:** It's personal, specific, and low-pressure. You're not blasting an offer to everyone — you're making a tailored recommendation.

**Step 4: The FOMO Trigger (Day 60+)**

If they haven't converted yet, create a time-boxed reason to act:

- "The monthly workshop recording expires in 48 hours for non-members"
- "Price increase next month — current price locked in if you join now"
- "We're capping the paid tier at 100 members to preserve quality"

These must be real. Fake urgency destroys trust. If you're actually raising prices or capping membership, announce it. If not, skip this step.

### Conversion Rate Benchmarks

| Community Type | Typical Free → Paid Conversion | What Good Looks Like |
|---------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| Professional network | 3-8% | 10%+ |
| Creator community | 5-10% | 15%+ |
| Coaching/mentorship | 8-15% | 20%+ |
| Course community | 10-20% | 25%+ |
| SaaS product community | 2-5% (of total users) | 5%+ |

**Important:** These are conversion rates of *total members*, not of *active members*. If you filter for only members who completed onboarding (40-70% of total), your conversion rate doubles or triples.

### What NOT to Do When Asking for Money

- **Don't turn your free community into a ghost town.** If you strip all value from free access after launching the paid tier, you break trust. The free tier should remain valuable — just limited.

- **Don't use guilt or obligation.** "If you really value this community, you'll support us" is manipulative and ineffective. Let the value speak for itself.

- **Don't make the first ask a big one.** Start with $9-19/month, not $99/month. You can always upsell later.

- **Don't hide the price.** Put your pricing somewhere visible. If people have to ask how much it costs, you've already lost their trust.

## Upsells: Courses, Coaching, and Digital Products

Membership revenue is reliable, but it's rarely enough on its own to build a full-time business — unless you have thousands of paid members. The real financial leverage comes from upsells: higher-ticket offers that serve your most engaged members.

### The Upsell Ladder

Your community should have a clear progression of offers, from lowest to highest price:

```
$0     Free community membership (reach)
↓
$29    Paid community membership (relationship)
↓
$197   Digital course (education)
↓
$497   Group coaching program (accountability)
↓
$1,497 One-on-one coaching (transformation)
```

Each level serves a different segment and a different need. Not everyone will climb the whole ladder. Most won't. But the ladder lets people grow with you instead of outgrowing you.

### Courses

Courses are the most common community upsell for a reason: they work. A course packages your knowledge into a high-value product that members can buy once.

**When to create a course:**
- You keep answering the same questions in your community
- Members regularly ask "do you have a guide on X?"
- You have a repeatable framework or methodology
- Your community has 100+ engaged members who trust you

**Pricing guidelines:**
| Course Type | Price Range | What's Included |
|-------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Mini-course | $27-47 | 3-5 lessons, quick wins |
| Full course | $97-297 | 8-12 modules, exercises, templates |
| Premium course | $397-997 | Everything above + group coaching calls |

**How to launch a course to your community:**

1. **Pre-sell before you build.** Announce the course, describe what it covers, and open a limited pre-sale at a discount. If nobody buys, don't build it.

2. **Give community members a discount.** "Community price: $197. Public price: $297." This rewards membership and creates urgency.

3. **Use the community for feedback.** Let early buyers shape the course content. Their questions become your lessons.

4. **Create a cohort experience.** Running the course as a live cohort (with a start date and group accountability) generates higher completion rates and better outcomes than an always-open self-study version.

### Coaching

Coaching is the highest-value upsell because it delivers the most transformation. It's also the most time-intensive, so you need to price it accordingly.

**Group coaching programs:**
- 4-8 weeks
- Weekly group calls
- Community support thread
- $297-997 per cohort
- Cap at 8-15 participants per cohort

**One-on-one coaching:**
- Ongoing monthly or 3-month packages
- Direct access, personalized guidance
- $500-2,500/month depending on your expertise and market
- Limit to 5-10 clients max (any more and quality drops)

**How to sell coaching to community members:**
- Identify members who are stuck at a specific level — they've consumed your content but haven't achieved the outcome
- Invite them to a discovery call: "I've noticed you've been working on [X] for a while. I think I could help you get there faster. Interested in a quick chat?"
- Use the call to understand their specific blockers and explain how your coaching addresses them

### Digital Products

Digital products are the easiest upsell because they don't require your ongoing time. Create once, sell forever.

**Product ideas by community type:**

| Community Type | Digital Product Ideas | Price Range |
|---------------|----------------------|-------------|
| Professional | Templates, frameworks, swipe files, checklists | $19-79 |
| Creative | Presets, assets, brushes, sound packs | $9-49 |
| Technical | Code snippets, boilerplates, API wrappers, configs | $29-99 |
| Educational | Worksheets, planners, study guides, cheat sheets | $9-39 |
| Health/Fitness | Meal plans, workout calendars, tracking sheets | $19-59 |

**Best practice for digital products:** Create them based on what your community already consumes for free. If you've shared a template in the community and 50 people downloaded it, that's a product. Package it, polish it, and sell it.

## Case Study: $29/Month to $1,200 MRR in 90 Days

*Note: This is an illustrative example based on common community monetization patterns, not a specific client case.*

Marco ran a free Discord server for freelance web designers. About 400 members. Active, helpful, good vibes. But Marco was spending 15 hours a week on it and making zero dollars.

He decided to launch a paid tier. Here's exactly what he did.

### Week 1: Setup

Marco created a three-tier structure using Discord roles and a Patreon integration:

| Tier | Price | Access |
|------|-------|--------|
| Free | $0 | #welcome, #general, #showcase, #free-resources |
| Pro | $29/mo | Everything free + #client-critique, #project-feedback, #tool-library, monthly group coaching call |
| Studio | $99/mo | Everything Pro + quarterly 1-on-1 strategy session, direct DM access to Marco, "Studio" role |

He set up Patreon to manage payments and assign Discord roles automatically via a bot.

### Week 2-3: Soft Launch

Marco didn't announce the paid tier to everyone at once. Instead, he:

1. Sent a DM to his 20 most active free members: "I'm testing a Pro tier. Would you be interested in beta access at $19/mo (50% off launch price)? Your feedback would help me shape it."

2. Got 12 signups from that DM alone. Gross revenue: $228/month from beta testers.

3. Used the beta period to refine the offer based on what testers actually used.

**Key insight:** Early adopters got a discount *and* got to shape the product. Marco got paying members AND product feedback at the same time.

### Week 4: Public Launch

Marco announced the paid tier to the full community with:
- A post explaining why he was launching paid tiers (sustainability, better resources, more time to invest in the community)
- A 48-hour launch discount: $19/mo instead of $29/mo for the first 100 members
- A clear description of what Pro included (with screenshots of the additional channels)
- A Q&A thread where people could ask questions

**Results from public launch:**
- 18 new Pro members ($522/mo)
- 3 new Studio members ($297/mo)
- Total: 33 paying members, $1,047/month MRR

### Week 5-12: Optimization

Over the next two months, Marco focused on retention and conversion:

- **Added tangible value to the Pro tier.** The monthly coaching call became the highest-attended benefit. Marco doubled down, inviting guest speakers and recording every session.
- **Created a "Pro member showcase."** Every month, he featured one Pro member's work in the main community. This created social proof and desire among free members.
- **Implemented a referral bonus.** Pro members got one month free for every 3 members they referred who converted to Pro.
- **Ran a "day in the life" post.** Marco shared what he was building for Pro members, making the tier feel exclusive and evolving.

### End of Month 12

Marco ended the first quarter at 42 paid members:
- 36 Pro members at $29/mo = $1,044/mo
- 6 Studio members at $99/mo = $594/mo
- Total MRR: $1,638/mo

That's $19,656 in annual recurring revenue from a community that was making $0 twelve weeks earlier.

### Key Takeaways From Marco's Launch

- **Start with your most engaged members.** Don't broadcast to everyone. Hand-pick your first 10-20 paid members. They'll give you better feedback and become your advocates.
- **Use beta pricing.** A discount for early adopters is fair and effective. They get a deal, you get validation.
- **Make the transition story authentic.** Marco was honest about why he needed to monetize. His community supported him because he didn't pretend it was something else.
- **Focus on one tier first.** Marco's Pro tier did the heavy lifting. Studio was a small bonus. Don't spread your energy across too many offers.
- **Build a retention loop.** The monthly coaching call became the reason people stayed. Every paid community needs a recurring touchpoint that members look forward to.

## Pricing Psychology: What Makes People Pay

Understanding a few psychological principles helps you price and position your offers better.

### The Decoy Effect

When you offer three tiers, the middle one should be the one you want people to buy. The top tier exists to make the middle tier look reasonable.

**Example:**
| Tier | Price | Perceived Value |
|------|-------|-----------------|
| Free | $0 | "Nice, but limited" |
| Pro | $29/mo | "Reasonable" ← buy this one |
| Studio | $99/mo | "Expensive, but I can see why Pro is better value" |

Nobody needs to buy the Studio tier for the pricing to work. Its job is to make $29/mo feel like a smart choice.

### Anchoring

People judge prices relative to what they see first. If you show them a $99/mo option first, $29/mo feels cheap. If you show them $9/mo first, $29/mo feels expensive.

Always lead with your highest price in a pricing table.

### The "Too Cheap" Problem

Pricing your community too low can actually reduce conversions. When something is too cheap, people question its quality. A $9/mo community might convert worse than a $29/mo community because $9 signals "low value."

**A rule of thumb:** If you're getting pushback on price, your value proposition might be unclear. If you're not getting *any* pushback on price, you might be charging too little.

### Annual vs. Monthly Billing

Offer both, but incentivize annual.

- Monthly: $29/mo
- Annual: $29/mo — but billed as $290/year (2 months free)

Annual billing improves cash flow and dramatically reduces churn. A member who pays annually is locked in for 12 months, giving you time to deliver value and earn renewal.

**Typical annual adoption rate:** 20-40% of paid members, depending on how strong the incentive is.

## What to Do This Week

1. **Choose your pricing model.** Subscription, tiered, or freemium + products. Pick one based on your community type and audience. Don't overthink it — you can change later.

2. **Set your price.** If you're unsure, start at $29/month. It's the sweet spot for most communities. Too low and you leave money on the table. Too high and you limit conversion.

3. **Write your paid tier description.** What does a paid member actually get? List 3-5 specific benefits. No vague language. Compare it to your free tier.

4. **Identify your first 10 potential paid members.** DM them. Offer beta pricing in exchange for feedback. Your first paid members are your most important — they'll shape the offer for everyone after them.

5. **Plan one upsell.** Pick one: a mini-course, a group coaching cohort, or a digital product. Sketch out what it would include and what you'd charge. You don't need to build it yet — just know what your first upsell will be.
