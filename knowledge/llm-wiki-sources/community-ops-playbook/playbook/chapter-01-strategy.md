# Chapter 1: Community Strategy & Positioning

**Purpose:** Most community launches fail before they start — not because of bad execution, but because nobody asked the hard questions upfront. This chapter helps you define why your community exists, who it's for, where it lives, and how you'll know it's working.

---

## Quick Wins — Do These Today

1. **Write your purpose sentence.** Complete: *"This community exists so that \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ can \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_."* If you can't finish this sentence, stop everything and figure it out.
2. **Pick one platform.** Start with Discord unless you have a compelling reason not to. Lowest cost to launch, easiest to change later.
3. **Define one North Star metric.** Not three. One. Write down what it is and why it matters.

## Start With Why (Not "Let's Build a Community")

"The answer is always community."

You've heard this a hundred times. It sounds good. It's also dangerously vague. "Building a community" is not a strategy — it's a tactic in search of a purpose. If you start there, you'll end up with a Discord server full of crickets and a lingering sense of failure.

Before you create a single channel, write a single post, or invite a single person, answer this question:

**What specific problem does this community solve that nothing else can?**

Here are three good answers:

- "Our SaaS product has a steep learning curve. Users who figure it out on their own stick around, but 60% churn before month three. A community where they can learn from each other and from us changes that."
- "I run a newsletter about indie game development. Paid subscribers get access to a private community where they can workshop their projects and get feedback from other devs."
- "I'm a fitness coach with 10,000 followers on Instagram. I can't deliver real coaching through posts and stories. A paid community lets me run challenges, give feedback, and build accountability groups."

Here's what's NOT a good answer:

- "Everyone else has a community, so we should too."
- "It'll be a good marketing channel."
- "We want to increase engagement."

These aren't *wrong*, exactly. They're incomplete. A community built on vague goals delivers vague results. A community built on a specific job to be done becomes indispensable.

**The litmus test:** Can you finish this sentence in one clear sentence?

> "This community exists so that \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ can \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_."

Example: "This community exists so that indie game developers can get honest playtest feedback before launch."

If you can't, stop. Work on the answer before you work on the server.

## Choose Your Platform: Discord vs Circle vs Skool vs Slack

Once you know *why* the community exists, the platform question gets easier. Each platform makes tradeoffs between control, engagement, monetization, and ease of use. Here's how they stack up.

### Discord

Best for: Real-time conversation, gaming/tech communities, large free communities with optional paid tiers.

Discord is the default for a reason. It's free, widely adopted, and built for high-frequency engagement. If your community thrives on chat — quick questions, casual conversation, voice hangouts — Discord handles that better than anything else.

**What works well:**
- Channels and categories let you organize conversations cleanly
- Voice channels for live events, co-working, or just hanging out
- Bot ecosystem (MEE6, Carl-bot, Dyno) for moderation, leveling, and engagement scoring
- Role-based permissions let you create paid tiers without leaving the platform
- 200 million monthly active users means most people already have an account

**What's tricky:**
- No built-in monetization. You'll need a third-party tool (Gumroad, Patreon, Ko-fi) or a bot to manage paid roles
- Can get noisy fast. Without strong moderation, quality drops
- Content disappears in scroll. Good luck finding that guide someone posted three days ago
- Not ideal for course-style content delivery

**When to pick it:** Your community is chat-forward, your audience skews younger, and you want zero upfront cost to start.

### Circle

Best for: Paid communities, course delivery, creator ecosystems.

Circle was built specifically for the "community as a product" model. It combines discussion spaces with content delivery, live streaming, and member management — all in one place.

**What works well:**
- Spaces function like mini-forums for different topics
- Native payment integration (Stripe) for subscription tiers
- Course content lives alongside discussion — no juggling Teachable + Discord
- Member profiles, direct messaging, and rich post formatting
- Clean, professional look that feels premium

**What's tricky:**
- Starts at $49/month for the basic plan (adds up fast)
- Less organic discovery — people don't "hang out" on Circle the way they do on Discord
- Smaller ecosystem means fewer integrations and bots
- Requires intentional design to keep engagement up

**When to pick it:** You're charging for access, you want everything under one roof, and your community values quality content over real-time chat.

### Skool

Best for: Course-led communities, gamification-heavy environments, solo creators.

Skool is the new kid that grew fast. It combines community with a learning management system and gamification — leaderboards, levels, achievements baked right in.

**What works well:**
- Gamification is native, not bolted on. Levels and leaderboards drive engagement automatically
- Calendar-based delivery for courses and events
- Clean, minimal UI that's easy for members to navigate
- Built-in monetization with Stripe
- Flat $99/month for unlimited members (no per-seat pricing)

**What's tricky:**
- Less flexible than Circle — you work within Skool's structure
- Forum-style discussions, not real-time chat. Feels more like a classroom than a conversation
- Gamification can feel gimmicky if your audience is professional/serious
- Smaller community of users means fewer best practices and templates

**When to pick it:** Your community centers around a course or program, you want gamification to drive participation, and you want simple pricing.

### Slack

Best for: B2B communities, professional networks, internal groups.

Slack wasn't made for communities — it was made for teams. But many B2B communities run on it anyway because their target audience already has it open all day.

**What works well:**
- Professional context — it feels like work, which means members treat it seriously
- Excellent channel organization and search
- Robust integrations with tools like Zapier, Google Drive, and CRM systems
- Threaded conversations stay readable

**What's tricky:**
- Free plan limits you to 90-day message history and 10 app integrations
- No built-in community features (no member profiles, no native monetization)
- Noise is a real problem in large Slack groups
- Not designed for content delivery or courses

**When to pick it:** Your members are already on Slack for work, your community is professional/B2B, and you need strong integrations with business tools.

### Quick Comparison

| Factor | Discord | Circle | Skool | Slack |
|--------|---------|--------|-------|-------|
| Best for | Chat communities | Paid communities | Course communities | B2B networks |
| Cost to start | Free | $49/mo | $99/mo | Free (limited) |
| Monetization | Third-party | Native | Native | Third-party |
| Content delivery | Weak | Strong | Strongest | Weak |
| Gamification | Via bots | Limited | Built-in | None |
| Learning curve | Low | Medium | Low | Medium |

### What About Running Multiple Platforms?

Don't. At least not at the start.

Every extra platform splits your attention, fragments your community, and multiplies your workload. Pick one primary platform and own it. You can always add a secondary channel later — a newsletter to recap discussions, a YouTube channel for recorded content — but your community needs one home.

## Set Goals and KPIs Before You Launch

If you don't know what success looks like, you won't recognize it when it arrives. And more importantly, you won't know what to optimize when things are going badly.

Define your goals in three layers:

### Layer 1: North Star Metric

One number that tells you the community is healthy. This is not vanity — it's the single metric that correlates most strongly with your business or mission outcome.

**Examples by community type:**

| Community Type | North Star | Why This One |
|---------------|-----------|-------------|
| Product support | Time-to-first-resolution | Faster answers = more retained users |
| Paid community | Retention rate (month 3) | If they stay 3 months, they'll stay 12 |
| Creator community | Active members / total members | Engagement rate predicts conversion |
| B2B network | Connections made per member | Network value = connections |

### Layer 2: Leading Indicators (Weekly)

These tell you if you're on track before the North Star moves:

- **New members joined** — Are you growing?
- **Messages sent** — Is conversation happening?
- **Daily active users / monthly active users (DAU/MAU)** — Are people coming back?
- **Posts with at least one reply** — Are conversations getting responses? (This matters more than total posts)
- **Onboarding completion rate** — Are new members getting past day one?

### Layer 3: Lagging Indicators (Monthly)

These confirm the trend:

- **Retention rate** — What % of members from 30/60/90 days ago are still active?
- **Free to paid conversion rate** (if applicable)
- **Member referrals per week** — Organic growth signal
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** — Would members recommend the community?

### Setting Your First Targets

Don't overthink this. Here are baseline targets for a community in its first 90 days:

| Metric | Baseline (First 90 Days) | Stretch Goal |
|--------|--------------------------|-------------|
| New members per week | 10-20 | 50+ |
| DAU/MAU ratio | 15-20% | 30%+ |
| Onboarding completion (Day 7) | 40% | 70%+ |
| Posts with replies | 60% | 80%+ |

These numbers shift depending on your platform and audience. A B2B Slack community will have different engagement patterns than a Skool course group. Track your own baselines starting week one, then compare against yourself, not against some industry average that may not apply.

## Member Personas: Who Are You Building For?

A community that serves everyone serves no one. The most common mistake is designing for "anyone interested in [topic]" — which is another way of saying no one in particular.

Define your core member persona before you launch. Here's a template:

> **[Name]** is a [demographic] who [struggles with X] and wants [outcome Y]. They [current behavior]. They'd join a community if it helped them [specific benefit].

**Example — Indie Game Dev Community:**

> **Alex** is a solo developer who finished their first game but struggled to get playtesters and feedback. They're active on Twitter and Reddit but haven't found a consistent group of peers. They'd join a community if it gave them regular, constructive playtest feedback and a network of other devs at their level.

Now design everything around Alex. The onboarding, the content, the engagement prompts — does it help Alex?

### Persona Dimensions

Define each persona across these dimensions:

1. **Goal** — What do they want to achieve by joining?
2. **Pain point** — What's frustrating them right now?
3. **Platform comfort** — Are they already on Discord? Do they hate learning new tools?
4. **Time availability** — Are they checking daily, or once a week?
5. **Willingness to pay** — $0, $9/month, $49/month?
6. **Contributor type** — Will they just consume, or do they want to help?

### Start With 1-3 Personas

You don't need ten personas. You need one to three, and one of them should be your "primary" persona — the person you'd design the whole community for if you could only serve one group.

For the indie game dev example, primary might be "solo devs seeking feedback." Secondary could be "small studio teams looking for networking." Tertiary might be "aspiring devs who haven't shipped anything yet."

Design for the primary. Accommodate the secondary. Let the tertiary find their own path.

## What to Do This Week

1. **Write your purpose sentence.** Complete: "This community exists so that \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_ can \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_." Put it somewhere you'll see every day.

2. **Pick one platform.** Use the comparison table above. If you're still unsure, start with Discord — lowest cost to launch, easiest to change later.
