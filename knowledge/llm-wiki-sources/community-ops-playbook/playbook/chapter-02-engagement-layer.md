# Chapter 2: The Engagement Layer System

**Purpose:** Most communities fail not because they lack members, but because they treat all members the same. A free member who just joined needs something completely different from a power user who's been around for a year. This chapter gives you a tiered system that serves each group appropriately — and naturally moves people up the ladder.

---

## Quick Wins — Do These Today

1. **Map your three tiers.** Even if you only launch with free, write down what Free, Paid, and VIP would include. This gives you a roadmap before you need it.
2. **Set up your engagement score sheet.** Create a spreadsheet with columns for Member Name, Posts, Replies, Reactions, Events, Referrals, and Total Score. Fill in your 10 most active members to test it.
3. **Define your top 3 free → paid triggers.** What moments will you use to invite members to upgrade? Write the actual message you'll send.

## Why One-Tier Communities Don't Work

Picture this: You open a Discord server. There are 800 members online. You type a question. And then you wait. And wait. Nobody responds because nobody feels responsible — it's a crowd, not a community.

That's the problem with flat communities. When everyone has the same access and the same experience:

- **New members feel lost.** They can't tell where to start or what matters.
- **Power users feel undervalued.** They're giving the most value but getting nothing extra.
- **Free riders face no friction.** There's no reason to contribute if you can consume indefinitely.
- **You burn out.** Without tiers, you're trying to serve everyone at once, which means you serve no one well.

The fix is an engagement layer system — three tiers that match member commitment with community access.

---

## The 3-Tier Model: Free → Paid → VIP

```
Free Tier (80% of members)
  │
  ▼  (value-driven upgrade path)
Paid Tier (15% of members)
  │
  ▼  (relationship-driven invitation)
VIP Tier (5% of members)
```

These numbers are rough guidelines, not gospel. A free community might have 95% free and 5% paid. A high-touch coaching community might flip that ratio. What matters is the structure: each tier has a clear purpose, a clear set of benefits, and a clear path to the next level.

### Tier 1: Free — The Front Door

**Purpose:** Value delivery and trust building. This is where people discover what you're about.

**What free members get:**

- Access to a limited set of channels or spaces (welcome, general discussion, announcements, a resources corner)
- Weekly digest or newsletter recapping top discussions
- Ability to read and react, with limited posting
- Access to public events (AMA, Q&A sessions)
- A clear taste of the community culture

**What free members should NOT get:**

- Access to premium content or archives
- Direct access to you or your team
- Advanced support or coaching
- Voting rights on community decisions

**The goal:** Free members should feel welcomed and valued, but also aware that there's more behind the door. This isn't about being stingy — it's about creating a reason to upgrade.

**Example setup — Discord:**

```
#welcome (free)
#general-chat (free)
#announcements (free)
#resources (free — curated starter guides)
#member-intros (free)
#premium-discussion (paid only)
#coaching-vault (paid only)
#vip-lounge (VIP only)
```

**Example setup — Circle:**

- Public spaces: Welcome, Community Board, Free Resources
- Private spaces (paid only): Deep Dives, Member Library, Monthly Q&A Archives
- Hidden spaces (VIP only): Strategy Calls, Private Feedback, VIP Network

### Tier 2: Paid — The Engine

**Purpose:** Revenue generation and deeper engagement. This is where your most active, committed members live.

**What paid members get:**

- Full access to all channels or spaces
- Exclusive content: deep dives, templates, frameworks
- Monthly group calls (Q&A, co-working, workshops)
- Access to an archive of past content and calls
- Priority support or feedback
- Ad-free experience (if applicable)
- Community directory or member listings

**How to price it:**

There's no magic number, but here's what works across community types:

| Community Type | Typical Price Point | What It Covers |
|---------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Professional network | $19-29/mo | Networking + monthly events + resource library |
| Creator community | $9-19/mo | Exclusive content + community access |
| Coaching/mentorship | $49-99/mo | Group coaching + feedback + accountability |
| Product support | Included in product ($50-200/mo SaaS) | Priority support + best practices sharing |

**Price anchoring trick:** If you offer a $29/mo plan and a $99/mo plan, put the $99/mo plan first in your pricing table. It makes $29/mo feel reasonable by comparison. This is called decoy pricing, and it works.

### Tier 3: VIP — The Inner Circle

**Purpose:** Deep relationships and community leadership. This is a small, curated group that helps you run the community and gets premium access in return.

**What VIP members get:**

- Direct access to you (private chat, monthly 1-on-1)
- Input on community direction (vote on features, content topics, events)
- Early access to new products, courses, or content
- VIP-only events (mastermind sessions, strategy days)
- Public recognition (badges, featured profiles, "community leader" status)
- A stake in the community's success (affiliate commissions, beta access, co-creation opportunities)

**How to select VIPs:**

Don't let people buy into VIP. Let them earn it. Set criteria:

- Active member for 3+ months
- Consistently helpful in discussions (not just posting, but *responding*)
- Attended at least 2 community events
- Referred at least 1 paid member
- Invitation from you or existing VIPs

VIP is a two-way relationship. You're not selling access — you're recruiting partners.

## How to Move Members Up the Tiers

The most common mistake is expecting members to upgrade on their own. They won't. You need a system that creates natural upgrade moments.

### Free → Paid Triggers

These are the moments when a free member is most likely to upgrade:

1. **They hit a content limit.** They've consumed all the free resources and want more. Make sure the free tier has a clear ceiling.

2. **They attend a public event.** Someone who shows up for a free AMA is warm. Pitch the paid tier at the end: "If you liked this, we go deeper every month in the paid community."

3. **They ask a specific question.** If a free member asks something you've already answered in detail inside the paid tier, offer a preview and a link: "Great question — I actually recorded a full walkthrough on this in the paid library. Here's a 2-minute clip. Want the full thing?"

4. **They hit day 14.** Timing matters. Day 14 after joining is a natural checkpoint. Send a message: "You've been here two weeks! Here's what you'd unlock if you upgraded..."

5. **They get a personal outreach.** A DM from you or a community manager checking in. Not a sales pitch — a genuine "how's it going?" that leads to "by the way, there's a whole deeper level if you're interested."

### Paid → VIP Triggers

VIP should feel exclusive. You invite, they don't apply.

- **After consistent helpful behavior:** "Hey, I've noticed you've been answering a ton of questions in the community. Would you be interested in joining the VIP group? It's my way of saying thanks, and I'd love your input on where we're heading."

- **After a referral:** "You've brought three people into the community this quarter. As a thank you, I'd like to invite you to the VIP tier."

- **After event participation:** "You've been to our last four monthly calls and your contributions are always top-notch. Would you like to join the VIP circle?"

## Engagement Scoring: Measure, Don't Guess

You can't manage what you don't measure. An engagement score gives you a single number that tells you how healthy a member's participation is. It lets you spot at-risk members before they go silent, identify potential VIPs before they get frustrated, and track whether your engagement efforts are working.

### The Simple Scoring Model

Assign points for actions, weighted by value:

| Action | Points | Why |
|--------|--------|-----|
| Join the community | 10 | Baseline |
| Complete profile/intro | 15 | Shows intent |
| Post a message | 3 per post | Participation |
| Reply to someone else | 5 per reply | Community behavior |
| Receive a reply | 2 per reply | Sparked conversation |
| React to a post | 1 | Low-effort engagement |
| Attend an event | 20 | High commitment |
| Refer a member | 50 | Growth contribution |
| Help another member (solved) | 30 | High value |

### Setting Thresholds

After 30 days, segment by score:

| Score Range | Segment | Action |
|-------------|---------|--------|
| 0-30 | New / At risk | Needs onboarding push |
| 31-100 | Active | Healthy, maintain engagement |
| 101-200 | Engaged | Ready for free → paid pitch |
| 200+ | Power user | VIP candidate |

These thresholds will vary by community. A B2B Slack group will have lower scores than a high-engagement Skool course. Track and adjust.

### How to Track It

- **Small communities (< 200):** A spreadsheet works fine. Update weekly.
- **Medium communities (200-1000):** Use a bot or integration. MEE6 and Carl-bot on Discord offer leveling systems you can repurpose for scoring.
- **Large communities (1000+):** A dedicated tool like Common Room, Orbit, or Vanilla Forums.

### What NOT to Score

- Time spent on platform (useless — someone could be logged in all day but never engage)
- Messages read (uncorrelated with value)
- Login count (people open apps out of habit, not intent)

Score for contribution and community health, not for passive consumption.

## Case Example: The Indie Dev Hangout — Sarah's Story

*Here's a story you can retell. It's a composite based on real community patterns, but it captures what happens when a well-meaning community hits the ceiling.*

### Before: The Free-for-All

Sarah loved building things. When she launched "The Indie Dev Hangout" — a free Discord server for solo game developers — she pictured a cozy workshop where indie devs traded tips and hyped each other's projects.

At first, it worked. Within three months she had 500 members. The #show-and-tell channel buzzed with pixel art and prototype clips. But soon, the cracks showed. The same 50 people carried every conversation. New members introduced themselves and never came back. Sarah personally answered every question because she felt guilty if she didn't. She was spending 10 hours a week keeping the lights on and making exactly $0.

"I had a community that was busy but not healthy," Sarah remembers. "I was the only person holding it together, and I was burning out."

### The Turning Point

Sarah realized her community had a structure problem, not a people problem. Everyone had the same experience — whether they'd joined yesterday or been a power user for a year. New members were overwhelmed. Her most helpful regulars had no reason to stick around. And there was zero revenue to justify the time she was putting in.

She needed a tiered system that served each group differently.

### After: The Tiered Transformation

Sarah restructured the server into three clear tiers.

**Free** members got #welcome, #general, #show-and-tell, and #free-resources — enough to get value and feel the culture.

**Paid members ($19/mo)** unlocked #code-reviews, #playtest-exchange, co-working voice channels, monthly coaching call archives, and a resource library of assets and templates. This became the engine of the community.

**VIP members ($49/mo or by invitation)** got a monthly mastermind call with Sarah, direct project feedback, and early access to her game dev courses. She capped it at 25 to keep it exclusive.

Then she set up MEE6 to track engagement scores automatically, added manual scoring for high-value actions like playtest participation and referrals, and reviewed the board every two weeks.

The upgrade moments came naturally:

- Free members hit a **resource ceiling** — five starter guides, then the full library required upgrading. This converted about 8% of free members.
- Developers hit the **playtest bottleneck** — they could show their game in #show-and-tell, but detailed playtest feedback required the paid tier. Playtest exchange became the #1 conversion driver.
- Every free member got a **day-14 DM** from Sarah: "Hey, just checking in — how's the game coming?" That personal touch turned conversations into upgrades.

For VIP, she waited 90 days and invited only the members who consistently gave thoughtful code reviews, organized spontaneous events, or referred others without being asked.

### The Results After 12 Months

| Metric | Before (Flat Community) | After (Tiered System) |
|--------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Total members | 500 | 1,200 |
| Paid members | 0 | 210 (17.5% conversion) |
| VIP members | 0 | 22 |
| Monthly revenue | $0 | $3,990 |
| Annual revenue | $0 | $47,880 |
| Churn rate (monthly) | N/A | 6% (improving to 4% by month 12) |
| Active engagement rate | ~10% | ~35% |

"Before, I was running a free support group," Sarah says. "After, I was running an actual business — and I was still putting in 10 hours a week. The difference is those 10 hours now generate $48K a year instead of leaving me exhausted."

### The Takeaway

Sarah's story isn't about adding paid tiers for the money. It's about matching the experience to the member. New devs got a welcoming on-ramp. Power users got deeper access and recognition. Sarah got a sustainable system that didn't depend on her being online 24/7.

You can retell it like this: *"I had a friend, Sarah, who ran a game dev Discord with 500 members and was burning out with nothing to show for it. She added three tiers — free, paid, VIP — set up engagement scoring, and a year later her community had grown to 1,200 members pulling in $48K a year. Same 10 hours a week, completely different outcome."*

## When to Add Tiers

Don't build all three tiers on day one. Start with:

1. **Free tier** — Launch this and validate that people actually want what you're building.
2. **Add paid tier** — Once you hit 100-200 engaged free members and you're confident in the value you're providing.
3. **Add VIP tier** — Once you have 20-30 paid members who've been active for 3+ months and you can identify your most valuable contributors.

Adding tiers too early creates complexity without revenue. Adding them too late misses opportunities. Watch for the signals — members asking "is there more?", people offering to pay, power users getting bored.
