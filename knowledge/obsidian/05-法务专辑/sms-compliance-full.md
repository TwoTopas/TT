# SMS Marketing: What You Need to Know Before You Start

**A plain-language guide for organizations considering text message outreach**

*Updated February 2026*

---

## Why This Document Exists

SMS marketing is one of the most effective communication channels available — open rates above 90%, near-instant delivery, and high engagement. But it's also one of the most heavily regulated.

Unlike email marketing, where you can set up a Mailchimp account and start sending in an afternoon, SMS marketing operates in an environment governed by federal law, industry trade associations, a national registration system, and mobile carrier enforcement. These aren't suggestions. Carriers now block all unregistered business text messages, and violations of the underlying federal law carry penalties of $500–$1,500 *per message*.

This guide is designed so that anyone considering SMS marketing — whether you're an executive director, a marketing manager, or a board member — can understand what you're getting into before you pick a vendor or sign a contract. It's organized into three sections:

1. **For the Decision-Maker** — The big picture: what SMS marketing involves, what it costs, and what commitments you're making.
2. **For Your Web Team** — The specific, non-negotiable changes your website needs before any SMS campaign can launch.
3. **For Legal and Compliance** — The regulatory stack, consent requirements, and liability exposure.

Each section is written to stand alone. Feel free to hand the relevant section to the relevant person on your team.

## Part 1: For the Decision-Maker

### What SMS Marketing Actually Involves

When most people think "I want to text our supporters," they picture something like email marketing — write a message, hit send, done. The reality is more complex.

Every business text message sent in the United States travels through a regulated ecosystem. Before your first message goes out, your organization must be:

- **Registered** with The Campaign Registry (TCR), a national database created by AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile to vet business texting
- **Compliant** with the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA), a federal law enforced by the FCC
- **Aligned** with CTIA Messaging Principles and Best Practices, the wireless industry's standards that carriers enforce through message filtering and blocking

Your SMS vendor (RumbleUp, Twilio, SimpleTexting, EZTexting, etc.) handles the TCR registration on your behalf. But *your organization* is responsible for having the compliant infrastructure in place — specifically your website, your privacy policy, and your opt-in process.

### The Registration Process

Here's what happens when you sign up with an SMS platform:

1. **Brand Registration** — Your organization is registered with TCR using your EIN, legal name, and website. TCR verifies your identity. Nonprofits need 501(c) status confirmable via IRS records.
2. **Campaign Registration** — Your specific use case (e.g., "program enrollment notifications") is registered. You provide sample messages, a description of your opt-in process, and links to your privacy policy and terms.
3. **Carrier Review** — AT&T, T-Mobile, and Verizon review your campaign. They check your website to verify that your privacy policy, terms of service, and opt-in mechanism exist and meet requirements.
4. **Approval or Rejection** — If everything checks out, your campaign is approved and you can start sending. If not, you're told what to fix. Common rejection reasons include missing privacy policy language, no visible opt-in mechanism on the website, or mismatched information.

This process typically takes 1–4 weeks. Rejections add time, because each resubmission goes back through the review queue.

### What It Costs

SMS marketing costs generally include:

- **Platform subscription** — Monthly fee to your SMS vendor (varies widely, $25–$500+/month depending on platform and volume)
- **Per-message costs** — Typically $0.01–$0.05 per message segment sent
- **TCR registration fees** — One-time brand registration ($4) and monthly campaign fee ($10/month for standard use cases) — usually passed through by your vendor
- **Your time** — Getting your website compliant, drafting privacy policy language, creating terms of service, and coordinating across your team

The platform and messaging costs are straightforward. The hidden cost is the compliance work — particularly if your website needs changes and your web team isn't familiar with these requirements.

### What You're Committing To

Before proceeding with SMS marketing, understand that you are committing to:

- **Maintaining compliant web pages** — Your privacy policy and terms must stay published and current as long as you're running SMS campaigns. If they go down or change in non-compliant ways, carriers can suspend your campaign.
- **Honoring opt-outs immediately** — When someone texts STOP, they must be removed. This is federal law, not a best practice.
- **Only messaging people who opted in** — You cannot import a list of phone numbers and start texting. Every recipient must have given explicit, documented consent to receive text messages from your organization specifically.
- **Record-keeping** — You should maintain records of how and when each person opted in. Proof of consent may be requested at any time by carriers or in the event of a legal dispute.

### Key Question Before You Proceed

Before selecting a vendor or starting any SMS project, ask yourself:

> *Do I have the ability to make changes to my organization's website — specifically to add or modify a privacy policy, publish terms of service, and embed an opt-in form?*

If the answer is "no" or "I'd have to go through a third party and I'm not sure they'll do it," that's the first problem to solve. No SMS vendor on any platform can launch your campaign without these website elements in place. This is not a vendor requirement — it's a carrier and federal requirement.

## Part 2: For Your Web Team

### Why You're Reading This

Someone in your organization wants to launch SMS/text message marketing. Before any messages can be sent, mobile carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile, Verizon) will review your website as part of the campaign registration process. If your website doesn't have the required elements, the campaign will be rejected.

These requirements are not specific to any SMS platform. They come from The Campaign Registry (TCR), the CTIA (the wireless industry trade association), and the Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA, federal law). Switching vendors doesn't change what your website needs.

### Website Checklist

Your website must have all of the following before SMS campaign registration is submitted:

#### 1. Privacy Policy (Separate, Publicly Accessible Page)

**Required URL structure:** `yoursite.com/privacy-policy/` (or similar — must be its own page, not buried inside another document)

**Must include this language** (or substantive equivalent):

- "No mobile information will be shared with third parties/affiliates for marketing/promotional purposes."
- "Opt-in data and consent will not be shared with any third parties."
- Description of what information is collected and how it is used
- Contact information for privacy inquiries

**Why:** TCR reviewers manually check your website for a privacy policy. Missing mobile-specific language is the #1 reason for campaign rejections.

#### 2. Terms of Service / Terms and Conditions (Separate, Publicly Accessible Page)

**Required URL structure:** `yoursite.com/terms/` (or similar — must be a separate page from the privacy policy)

**Must include:**

- Name of the organization/program
- Description of messages the subscriber will receive
- Message frequency disclosure (e.g., "Message frequency may vary")
- "Message and data rates may apply"
- Opt-out instructions (e.g., "Reply STOP to unsubscribe")
- HELP/customer care instructions (e.g., "Reply HELP for help or contact [email/phone]")
- Contact information

**Why:** The SMS opt-in form must link separately to both a Terms page and a Privacy Policy page. If these are combined into one page, some carrier reviews will reject the campaign because the form can't link to two distinct URLs.

#### 3. SMS Opt-In Form

**Must include:**

- A clear description of what the user is signing up for
- A checkbox or affirmative action (not pre-checked) that the user must complete
- Disclosure text near the form that includes:
  - Program name or description
  - "Message frequency may vary" (or specific frequency)
  - "Message and data rates may apply"
  - Links to both the Terms of Service AND the Privacy Policy (as separate, clickable links)
- The form must collect, at minimum, a mobile phone number

**Why:** The opt-in form is where consent is captured. TCR and carriers verify that the form exists, includes required disclosures, and links to both required pages. An opt-in form without these elements is treated as if no opt-in exists.

#### 4. What NOT to Do

- **Don't put blanket consent language in the site footer.** A statement like "by visiting this website you consent to receiving text messages" is the opposite of explicit opt-in. It could actually be used as evidence of non-compliance.
- **Don't combine Terms and Privacy Policy on one page** if your opt-in form needs to link to both separately.
- **Don't use a pre-checked checkbox.** The user must take an affirmative action to opt in.
- **Don't hide the opt-in form or required disclosures.** They must be visible without scrolling past the form or clicking through to find them.

### Summary for Quick Reference

| Element | Required? | Must Be Separate Page? | Linked from Opt-In Form? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Privacy Policy | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Terms of Service | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Opt-In Form with Disclosures | Yes | N/A (embedded on a page) | N/A |
| STOP/HELP Instructions | Yes (in Terms + in messages) | No | No |

## Part 3: For Legal and Compliance

### The Regulatory Stack

SMS marketing in the United States is governed by four overlapping layers of authority. Understanding this stack is important because it means compliance isn't optional at any level — and it explains why "just switching platforms" doesn't solve compliance problems.

#### Layer 1: TCPA — Telephone Consumer Protection Act (Federal Law)

- **Authority:** Federal Communications Commission (FCC)
- **Enacted:** 1991, updated to include text messaging in 2012
- **Enforcement:** Government enforcement + private right of action (individuals can sue)
- **Penalties:** $500 per unsolicited message; $1,500 per willful violation
- **Key requirements:** Prior express written consent before sending marketing messages; honoring opt-out requests; maintaining do-not-call lists

The TCPA is the legal foundation. Everything else builds on top of it. Class action lawsuits under the TCPA are common and have resulted in settlements in the tens of millions of dollars against companies of all sizes.

#### Layer 2: CTIA Messaging Principles and Best Practices (Industry Standards)

- **Authority:** CTIA (Cellular Telecommunications Industry Association)
- **Current version:** May 2023
- **Enforcement:** Voluntary standards, but carriers enforce them through TCR and message filtering
- **Key requirements:** Clear and conspicuous calls-to-action (opt-in disclosures); program name, frequency, and "message and data rates may apply" in CTA; STOP/HELP keyword support; publicly accessible privacy policy; opt-in confirmation messages

The CTIA guidelines operationalize the TCPA's broad requirements into specific, checkable items. When a carrier says "your privacy policy is missing required language," they're referencing CTIA standards.

#### Layer 3: The Campaign Registry (TCR)

- **Authority:** Created jointly by AT&T, Verizon, and T-Mobile
- **Function:** National registration database for business text messaging campaigns
- **Enforcement:** Campaign approval/rejection; ongoing monitoring
- **Key requirements:** Brand identity verification (EIN match); campaign use case registration; website verification (privacy policy, terms, opt-in mechanism); sample message review

TCR is the gatekeeper. Your SMS vendor (called a Campaign Service Provider, or CSP) submits your registration to TCR. TCR verifies your brand identity and reviews your campaign. Carriers then review and approve or reject based on TCR's findings and their own policies. As of February 2025, carriers block all unregistered business text message traffic on 10DLC numbers.

#### Layer 4: Individual Carrier Policies

- **Authority:** AT&T, T-Mobile, Verizon (individually)
- **Enforcement:** Message filtering, blocking, fines, campaign suspension
- **Penalties:** Up to $10 per non-compliant message (carrier-imposed)
- **Key requirements:** Each carrier may impose additional requirements beyond TCR/CTIA minimums; content filtering for prohibited content categories; throughput limits based on trust score

Carriers are the final authority on whether your messages are delivered. Even a TCR-approved campaign can have messages filtered or blocked if carriers detect policy violations.

### Consent Requirements in Detail

The TCPA requires "prior express written consent" for marketing messages. Under current FCC rules, this means:

- The consent must be **in writing** (electronic signatures, including web form submissions, qualify)
- The consent must be **clear and conspicuous** — the person must know they're agreeing to receive text messages
- The consent must be **specific to your organization** — you cannot buy a list of people who consented to receive texts from a different organization
- The consent must be **voluntary** — you cannot require consent as a condition of purchase or service (with limited exceptions)
- The consent must include disclosure of the **type and frequency** of messages
- You must maintain **records** of consent

Consent obtained through a compliant web form (with proper disclosures, unchecked checkbox, and links to terms/privacy policy) satisfies these requirements.

### What About Other Number Types?

Organizations sometimes ask whether they can avoid these requirements by using a different type of phone number. The short answer is no:

- **Short codes** (5–6 digit numbers): Subject to CTIA Short Code Monitoring Program, which has *stricter* requirements than 10DLC. Cost $1,000+/month.
- **Toll-free numbers** (800, 888, etc.): Subject to a separate toll-free verification registry with functionally identical requirements.
- **Personal cell phones:** TCPA still applies. Not scalable. Carriers flag high-volume sending from personal numbers as spam.

Every path to business text messaging requires: a privacy policy, opt-in consent, opt-out mechanisms, and terms disclosure. The registration body varies, but the requirements don't.

### Liability Considerations

Organizations should be aware of the following:

- **TCPA class actions are common** — Plaintiffs' attorneys actively seek TCPA violations because of the per-message statutory damages and the private right of action. Cases have been filed against organizations of all sizes, including nonprofits.
- **"I didn't know" is not a defense** — The TCPA is a strict liability statute for certain provisions. Intent is not required for basic violations.
- **Carrier fines are separate from legal liability** — A carrier imposing a $10/message fine for policy violations is independent of any TCPA lawsuit. You can face both.
- **Your vendor won't indemnify you** — SMS platform terms of service universally place compliance responsibility on the customer (your organization). The platform provides tools; you're responsible for using them correctly.

### Recommended Steps Before Launch

1. **Consult legal counsel** familiar with TCPA compliance before launching any SMS program
2. **Audit your website** against the checklist in Part 2
3. **Draft compliant privacy policy and terms** with attorney review
4. **Document your opt-in process** — screenshot your form, save the page URL, and keep records of when and how each subscriber opted in
5. **Train staff** on opt-out handling and consent requirements
6. **Review your vendor's compliance documentation** — understand what they handle and what falls on you
